# comBO: A Combined Human Bone and Lympho-Myeloid Bone Marrow Organoid for Preclinical Modeling of Hematopoietic Disorders

The bone marrow microenvironment is an extraordinarily complex niche composed of endothelial cells, mesenchymal stromal populations, adipocytes, and osteogenic lineages, all of which collectively regulate hematopoiesis. Reconstructing this diversity in vitro has remained a persistent challenge because many systems incompletely represent multilineage hematopoiesis, lack coordinated adipogenic and osteogenic compartments, or are difficult to scale for preclinical testing. In this context, the recently reported ComBO platform by Yuqi Shen, Camelia Benlabiod, and colleagues (senior author Abdullah O. Khan) is an important technical and conceptual advance.

ComBO introduces a combined human bone and lympho-myeloid marrow organoid designed to support both lymphopoiesis and myelopoiesis while retaining structural and stromal complexity. The developmental strategy uses staged cytokine exposure and controlled oxygen conditions to guide niche assembly: mesoderm induction is followed by hydrogel embedding and a lymphoid induction phase in which IL-7 plays a central role. By day 21 to 35, the system shows a CD34-positive endothelial architecture supported by CD271-positive stromal populations. Lymphoid progenitors are enriched in regions that also exhibit early osteogenic signals, and classical histological readouts demonstrate progressive mineralization. Together, these observations indicate that ComBO captures more of the marrow’s compositional breadth than many earlier models.

Single-cell transcriptomics further strengthens this claim. At both intermediate and later time points, the organoids contain a broad hematopoietic spectrum, including myeloid and lymphoid progenitors, monocyte/macrophage populations, dendritic cells, neutrophils, erythroid cells, megakaryocytic compartments, and HSPC-associated states. The stromal landscape is similarly diverse, with endothelial subtypes, mesenchymal stromal cells, CAR-like populations, and adipocyte/osteoblast-lineage states. Importantly, signatures associated with hematopoietic maturation increase over time, suggesting that the system is not static but developmentally progressive.

One of the most practical contributions of the study is scalability. The authors use granular microgels to generate compacted organoids in multiwell formats without major loss of cellular complexity, which substantially improves throughput and experimental reproducibility. This matters for translation: technically elegant organoids often fail to become widely useful because they cannot be produced robustly at scale. In contrast, ComBO appears deliberately engineered for broader preclinical adoption.

Functionally, the platform supports extended maintenance of hematopoietic progenitors through serial reseeding, with preserved lymphoid and myeloid differentiation capacity. It also demonstrates disease relevance in a clinically difficult context: primary CD138-positive multiple myeloma cells engraft and expand in the organoids, accompanied by measurable microenvironmental remodeling. Multi-omic analyses indicate broad niche perturbation, including inflammatory and metabolic pathway activation, altered cellular abundances, and intensified cytokine communication. MIF-centered signaling emerges as a key inflammatory axis, and both pharmacologic inhibition and knockout experiments reduce components of this inflammatory phenotype, providing a clear mechanistic anchor rather than purely descriptive profiling.

At the same time, several issues deserve careful scientific scrutiny. The osteogenic component, while present, appears temporally delayed and less robust at earlier stages. The data primarily support detection of early osteogenic markers, while convincing evidence for potentially mature osteoblast states remains limited and osteocyte-like populations are not clearly represented. Although mineralization becomes clearer with prolonged culture, the overall pattern is more consistent with gradual self-organized maturation than strongly directed osteogenesis. This interpretation is plausible given that canonical osteo-inductive supplements are not emphasized as dominant drivers in the protocol. Matrix context and long-term remodeling likely contribute substantially to the eventual phenotype.

There are also interpretation-level ambiguities in the lineage narrative. Some genes discussed as osteoblast-defining are not strictly lineage-restricted, and selected text-to-figure attributions appear stronger than the transcriptional evidence alone would support. This does not negate the model’s value, but it argues for more conservative marker language and deeper orthogonal validation when claiming osteogenic state identity. In parallel, the need for periodic reseeding to maintain hematopoietic compartments suggests that long-term autonomous niche stability remains a work in progress. In vivo functional repopulation assays of long-term hematopoietic potential would further clarify how closely the in vitro CD34-associated states map to durable stem cell function.

Overall, ComBO is a technically impressive and highly promising platform for preclinical marrow biology. Its strongest contributions are the integration of multilineage hematopoiesis, scalable manufacturing logic, and actionable disease-modeling capacity in multiple myeloma. The osteogenic component is compelling but not yet fully resolved mechanistically, and this should be a central focus of future iterations. Even with these limitations, ComBO represents a meaningful step toward physiologically richer and experimentally practical human marrow organoid systems.
